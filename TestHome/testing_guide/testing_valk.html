Day-To-Day Structure

Each day consists of 8 working hours. The daily structure for EVE is as such:

Morning - 3.5 hours
15 min Stand-up in the morning
15 min break at 10:00

Afternoon - 3.75 hours
15 min break at 15:00

Daily Routine

  • Tick Tock – check-in to tick tock and ensure your daily activities are complete
  • Skype – sign into Skype and check important test channels
  • Email – ensure you’re signed into Outlook
  • Lync/Skype for business – ensure you’re signed into Lync
  • Group Chat – open up and sign into group chat
  • HMD – it’s a good idea to remind your Lead or Senior to pass out the Oculus/PS headsets.
  • Prepare for daily Stand Up
At the end of the day

  • If going on holiday, update your time entry activities for the days you’ll be off and get your team lead to check them before you leave
  • Shut down your computer – help protect the environment!

Good Practices

  • Maintain a list of personal defects for quick reference
  • Check on the status of own defects daily
  • Save an up-to-date DxDiag each week
  • When on a public test server - always clean up after yourself (remove wrecks, cargo containers, don't leave ships floating about in space)
  • Slash commands should only be used in the Slash Console
  • Ocassionally free up some HDD space by deleting old log server logs and screenshots
  • Save copies of entered defects with screenshots and logs - for easily offline reference
  • If you are being stymied by a script, test around it by UBERTESTing
  • Question Everything!
    • When you see something odd, always investigate it. Assuming is not the same as knowing
  • Communication is KEY
    • When in doubt, ASK
    • If in doubt, BUG IT

UBERTEST

UBERTEST is an acronym designed to apply to different facets of QA testing. The acronym breaks down like this:

Usability – How easy and intuitive things are to use.
Boundaries – Limitations, going over them or trying to.
Equivalence Partitioning – Testing extremes and the middle, instead of all possibilities, to save time.
Results – Does each action result in the correct outcome.
Transition – Moving from one screen/state/situation to another in different ways.
Error Recovery – Does the program handle things well when things go wrong.
Stress – How things scale, doing things en masse or repetitively.
Technical Requirements – Do things work acceptably on min specs or low settings and with bad connections etc.

You should use it as a framework to apply to different situations when you are ad-hoc/exploratory testing. Remember, UBERTEST isn't just a set of guidelines - it's a state of mind.

Shortcuts

As you can imagine, our desktops are littered with shortcuts and files for easy access. We all have our own little systems, but there are a few shortcuts you'll definitely be wanting to help you in your testing ways.

Obviously, the most important shortcuts are the ones to the game itself. We have multiple clients installed, each directing to a different server depending on their shortcut target.

Useful file directory shortcuts include:

  • CCP Tester Access - All CCP related files on the PTW Network
  • Reporting - A direct link to the EVE Reporting folder on the network
  • Common Files - Some testers find it helpful to have a folder with shortcuts to each Client's common.ini file, usually located in C:\Program Files\CCP\[Client]\common.ini as these files tell you the branch and build of the client
  • FRAPS Movies - to easily find any files you record in FRAPS
  • Program Files CCP - where all your clients are saved on your PC

Essential programs include:

  • FRAPS - for recording videos of issues in game
  • Windows Movie Maker - for editing aforementioned videos
Finally it's probably a good idea to keep your DxDiag on your desktop as you'll upload it to every defect. You may also find it useful to keep shortcuts to your own personal defect and DNA lists. A blank New Text Document can come in handy whenever you're entering a defect that you've written up in word -the CCP defect tracker sometimes doesn't like you copying straight from word, so just copy your defect text into a blank .txt file then copy from there - it's a faff but it gets the job done!
